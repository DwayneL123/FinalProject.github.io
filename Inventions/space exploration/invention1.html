Sputnik 1

The First Satellite

Overview

Sputnik 1, a polished aluminum sphere with four antennas, was the first artificial satellite to enter Earth orbit. It was launched by the Soviet Union in 1957 and transmitted simple radio signals that could be received around the world. It was built to study atmospheric density and test the propagation of radio signals in space, a scientific milestone. It also symbolized Soviet technological prowess during the International Geophysical Year.

It consisted of a pressurized metal sphere, 58 cm in diameter, containing a radio transmitter powered by a chemical battery. Four whip-like antennas extended outward to transmit signals from orbit. The rocket was launched on October 4, 1957 and operated continuously for 21 days before the batteries ran out. It remained in orbit until re-entering Earth orbit and disintegrating on January 4, 1958.

The satellite was developed by Soviet scientists led by Sergei Korolev, influenced by earlier concepts of Konstantin Tsiolkovsky. It was part of the wider Sputnik program. It marked the dawn of space exploration and ignited the Space Race between superpowers. Its success spurred rapid advancements in science, technology, and global communication.
