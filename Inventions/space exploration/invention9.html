The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)

Overview

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is the most powerful space observatory to date, with the goal of exploring the universe using infrared light. JWST, which was launched on December 25, 2021, will orbit the second Lagrange point (L2) about a million miles from Earth, allowing it to investigate the universe's oldest galaxies, exoplanets, and cosmic events with unprecedented detail. The JWST's mission is to investigate the universe's origins, with a focus on four major areas: the first light after the Big Bang, the assembly of early galaxies, star and planet formation, and the search for exoplanets and the possibility of life beyond Earth. It tries to unearth previously unknown facts about the universe by observing at infrared wavelengths.

NASA, the European Space Agency (ESA), and the Canadian Space Agency (CSA) collaborated to develop JWST, which involved more than 300 universities and organizations. It consists of a massive 6.5-meter segmented mirror, four major scientific equipment, and a tennis court-sized sunshield to protect it from the heat of the Sun, allowing the telescope to detect subtle infrared signals. The James Webb Space Telescope was launched on December 25, 2021, and completed its 30-day voyage to the L2 point on January 24, 2022. Its scientific mission began in 2022, following a series of calibration tests.

NASA, the European Space Agency (ESA), and the Canadian Space Agency (CSA) collaborated to develop the JWST. It was created with considerable contributions from numerous institutions, contractors, and over 300 colleges and organizations worldwide. JWST is transforming our view of the cosmos, allowing us to learn more about galaxies, star formation, and the possibility of life on other worlds. Its ability to record high-resolution infrared photos and data contributes to a variety of fields, including astrophysics, planetary science, and the search for extraterrestrial life, helping both scientific communities and the public's understanding of space.
