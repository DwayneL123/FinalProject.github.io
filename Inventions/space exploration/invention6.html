Mars Rovers

Curiosity, Perseverance

Overview

Mars rovers are robotic vehicles dispatched by NASA to study the Martian terrain. They are outfitted with scientific instruments to investigate the planet's geology, environment, and possibility for past or present life. To examine the Martian surface, analyze rocks and soil, look for evidence of ancient water and life, and gather data for future human trips to Mars.

Mars rovers are constructed using long-lasting materials and advanced engineering, including wheels for terrain mobility, scientific instruments (e.g., spectrometers, cameras, drills), solar panels or nuclear power sources, and communication systems for sending data back to Earth. Each each generation of rovers has grown in size, complexity, and capability. The first successful rover, Sojourner, landed in 1997 after being launched in 1996. It was succeeded by Spirit and Opportunity in 2004, Curiosity in 2012, and Perseverance in 2021.

NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), a part of the California Institute of Technology, designed and operates the Mars rovers. The Mars rovers have considerably aided our understanding of Mars. They've found traces of ancient water, identified chemical molecules, investigated radiation levels for human flight, and tested technology like as oxygen synthesis on Mars. Their discoveries sparked scientific curiosity, educational involvement, and advances in robotics and space travel.
