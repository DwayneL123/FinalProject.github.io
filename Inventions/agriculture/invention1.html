Short Description

A circular frame—solid, spoked, or partly solid—designed to rotate on an axle, enabling rotary motion. The wheel can be attached to carts, gears, and other machinery to convert linear force into circular motion. It remains one of the simplest yet most powerful mechanical inventions in history.

Purpose

To facilitate transportation, irrigation, pottery making, and mechanical operations through controlled rotary motion. By reducing friction and distributing weight, wheels made it easier to move heavy loads and perform repetitive tasks. They also allowed for greater efficiency in early machines and tools.

How It Is Made

Early wheels were solid wooden disks made from planks tightly bound together and trimmed into a circular shape. As technology progressed, wheels were crafted with spokes and iron reinforcements, including iron hubs and rims, which added strength and longevity. These advancements allowed wheels to support heavier loads and move faster with less material.

When It Is Made

The earliest known wheels date back to around 3500 BC in Mesopotamia (Sumer), often associated with early carts. By around 2000 BC, spoked wheels began to appear, marking a significant improvement in mobility and vehicle design. These developments spread quickly across ancient civilizations, influencing trade and warfare.

Who Made It

The invention of the wheel is credited to the ancient Mesopotamian people, particularly those in the Sumerian civilization. These early innovators applied their knowledge of woodworking and mechanics to solve practical problems in transportation and labor. Their ingenuity laid the foundation for countless technological advancements.

Contribution To Society

The wheel revolutionized transport, agriculture, and manufacturing, enabling people to travel longer distances and move goods efficiently. It also allowed for the creation of water-lifting devices, grain mills, and other machinery that transformed economies and daily life. Later, the wheel’s principles powered industrial machines and engines, playing a vital role in the Industrial Revolution.
