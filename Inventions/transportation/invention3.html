Short Description

A type of engine in which fuel combustion occurs inside the engine itself, producing hot gases that expand and generate mechanical work. Unlike steam engines, it does not require an external boiler. This design allows for compact, powerful engines ideal for mobile applications.

Purpose

To convert chemical energy from fuel into mechanical energy for powering vehicles, machinery, aircraft, and rockets. It enables independent, portable energy sources essential for modern transportation and mechanized equipment. Internal combustion engines are used everywhere from lawn mowers to fighter jets.

How It Is Made

Constructed with essential components such as cylinders, pistons, crankshafts, camshafts, fuel delivery systems (like carburetors or fuel injectors), and spark plugs (in gasoline engines). These components work together in a cycle—intake, compression, combustion, and exhaust—to convert fuel into motion. Diesel engines differ by using compression ignition rather than spark ignition, offering higher efficiency for heavy-duty uses.

When It Is Made

The first practical internal combustion engines were developed in the mid-19th century and saw rapid innovation through the early 20th century. By the late 1800s, internal combustion engines began powering early automobiles, and by the early 1900s, they became standard in transportation. Continued advancements improved efficiency, power output, and fuel versatility.

Who Made It

Étienne Lenoir (first practical engine, 1859) Nikolaus Otto (four-stroke engine, 1876) Gottlieb Daimler and Karl Benz (automotive applications) Rudolf Diesel (diesel engine, 1897) Others like Nicéphore Niépce and Ole Evinrude contributed to specific innovations.

Contribution To Society

The internal combustion engine revolutionized transportation by making automobiles, airplanes, and motorcycles practical and widespread. It enabled global trade, personal mobility, military logistics, and industrial automation. As a cornerstone of the 20th-century technological leap, it shaped urbanization, globalization, and the modern energy economy.
