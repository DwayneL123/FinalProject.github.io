Short Description

DNA sequencing is a laboratory technique used to determine the exact order of the four chemical bases (adenine, thymine, cytosine, and guanine) in a DNA molecule. It reveals the genetic blueprint of living organisms.

Purpose

The purpose of DNA sequencing is to understand genetic information, identify gene mutations, study inherited diseases, support forensic investigations, improve agriculture, and advance medical research, including personalized medicine and drug development.

How It Is Made

DNA sequencing involves extracting DNA from a sample, preparing it with enzymes or chemical treatments, and reading the sequence using machines. Techniques like Sanger sequencing and next-generation sequencing (NGS) convert biological material into digital data showing the DNA base order.

When It Is Made

The first method for DNA sequencing was developed in 1977. The Human Genome Project, launched in 1990 and completed in 2003, was a major milestone that mapped all the genes in human DNA using advanced sequencing technologies.

Who Made It

Dr. Frederick Sanger developed the first widely-used DNA sequencing method, now known as Sanger sequencing, in 1977. His work earned him a Nobel Prize and laid the foundation for modern genetic science.

Contribution To Society

DNA sequencing has revolutionized biology and medicine by enabling scientists to study diseases at the genetic level, trace ancestry, identify genetic disorders, solve crimes, and create genetically modified crops. It continues to drive breakthroughs in healthcare and biotechnology, making it one of the most important innovations in science.
