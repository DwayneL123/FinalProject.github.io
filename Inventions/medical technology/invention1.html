Short Description

One key method for eliminating diseases is vaccination. This process was developed to prevent widespread transmission of illnesses. It helps protect individuals and communities from dangerous infections.

Purpose

Vaccination aims to stop diseases from spreading by giving people immunity before they come into contact with a virus or bacteria. This helps reduce outbreaks and protects vulnerable populations, including children, the elderly, and those with weakened immune systems.

How It Is Made

When introduced into the body, it trains the immune system to recognize and remember the pathogen. That way, if a person is later exposed to the actual disease, the body can fight it off more effectively. This process of vaccination was developed to prevent the spread of diseases and reduce their impact on human populations.

When It Is Made

Dr. Edward Jenner developed the first successful vaccine in 1796 to fight smallpox, which had existed for centuries. The earliest evidence of smallpox comes from a description of a similar illness in China during the 4th century CE.

Who Made It

Dr. Edward Jenner created the first successful vaccine using material from cowpox to fight smallpox, a once-deadly disease that caused millions of deaths.

Contribution To Society

Smallpox killed about 3 out of every 10 people who contracted it. Survivors often had lasting, sometimes severe, scars. In 1959 the World Health Organization (WHO) began a global vaccination campaign to eradicate smallpox. Though the first attempt failed due to limited funding and vaccine supply, efforts continued. Smallpox was ultimately eradicated, with no new cases since 1977. In October 1979, the WHO celebrated the achievement, and on May 8, 1980, the 33rd World Health Assembly officially recognized the eradication. Many consider the invention of the smallpox vaccine the most significant accomplishment in international public health.
