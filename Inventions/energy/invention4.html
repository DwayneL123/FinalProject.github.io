Short Description

The steam turbine is a heat engine that converts thermal energy from high-pressure steam into rotational motion. This rotary power drives electrical generators, making steam turbines essential in power plants fueled by fossil fuels, nuclear energy, and concentrated solar power.

Purpose

Steam turbines primarily convert steamâ€™s thermal energy into mechanical rotational energy, which drives generators to produce electricity in power plants fueled by gas, coal, nuclear, or solar heat. They are also used in applications like district cooling systems.

How It Is Made

A steam turbine uses heat to boil water, producing high-pressure steam that flows over blades, converting thermal energy into kinetic energy and causing the rotor to spin. The blade design controls steam speed, direction, and pressure to maximize efficiency. The spinning shaft drives a generator to produce electricity.

When It Is Made

The modern and practical version of the steam turbine was invented by Charles Parsons in 1884.

Who Made It

Charles Parsons is credited with inventing the first practical reaction turbine design in 1884.

Contribution To Society

Parsons' invention of the steam turbine revolutionized both power generation and marine transport. It made the production of cheap and abundant electricity possible, which was fundamental to the development of modern industrial society. Today, steam turbines remain a vital technology, responsible for a significant portion of global electricity generation in thermal power stations.
