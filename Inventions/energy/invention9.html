Lithium Ion Batteries

Short Description

The lithium-ion (Li-ion) battery is the leading rechargeable battery today, powering many portable devices and electric vehicles. Although rechargeable batteries date back to 1859, Li-ion technology was developed in the 1970s and first commercialized in 1991. Its high voltage and energy density drive its widespread use.

Purpose

The primary purpose of lithium-ion batteries is to offer a lightweight, high-energy, rechargeable power source for portable electronics, electric vehicles, and other applications needing efficient energy storage.

How It Is Made

During discharge, lithium ions move from the anode to the cathode through the electrolyte, creating electric current. The anode stores ions between graphite layers. Common materials are lithium cobalt oxide (cathode) and graphite (anode).

When It Is Made

Research on lithium-ion batteries began in the 1970s, with the first commercial cell released in 1991. In 2019, Goodenough, Whittingham, and Yoshino won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for their key contributions.

Who Made It

The modern lithium-ion battery was developed thanks to John B. Goodenough, M. Stanley Whittingham, and Akira Yoshino, who jointly won the 2019 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for their work.

Contribution To Society

Lithium-ion batteries have transformed portable electronics by enabling smaller, lighter, and more powerful devices. They are essential for electric and hybrid vehicles, providing a cleaner alternative to gasoline. Their high energy density and rechargeability make them vital for many applications, driving technological progress and sustainable energy transition.
