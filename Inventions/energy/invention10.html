Short Description

A fuel cell is an electrochemical device that generates electricity by reacting a fuel, like hydrogen, with an oxidant, such as oxygen. When using hydrogen, the only byproducts are electricity, water, and heat, making it a clean energy source. Fuel cells can power everything from small devices to large power plants and use various fuels.

Purpose

Fuel cells primarily provide clean, efficient electricity for transportation, buildings, and energy storage. They offer a more efficient and low-emission alternative to traditional combustion power generation.

How It Is Made

A fuel cell works by supplying fuel (like hydrogen) to the anode and oxygen to the cathode, separated by an electrolyte. At the anode, hydrogen splits into protons and electrons; electrons flow through a circuit creating electricity, while protons pass through the electrolyte to combine with oxygen and electrons at the cathode, producing water and heat.

When It Is Made

Who Made It

Sir William Robert Grove invented the first fuel cell in 1839, laying the groundwork for fuel cell technology. Though his device had limited power, it established key principles later developed by many scientists and engineers.

Contribution To Society

Hydrogen fuel cells provide clean energy with zero emissions, especially for transportation. They offer efficient, quiet power for buildings and grid storage. With growing use and lower costs, they are key to a sustainable energy future.
