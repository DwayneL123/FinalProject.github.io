Carbon Fiber

Composite Material

Overview

Carbon fiber is a composite material consisting of thin, strong crystalline filaments of carbon. These filaments, often finer than a human hair, gain their strength by being twisted together like yarn. This yarn can then be woven into fabrics, and to create rigid structures, it can be layered over a mold and infused with a resin or plastic matrix. Carbon fiber's exceptional strength-to-weight ratio and stiffness have made it invaluable in various high-performance applications. Carbon fiber is used to provide a lightweight material with exceptional strength and stiffness for applications where these properties are critical. This includes aerospace (aircraft structures), automotive industry (lightweighting for fuel efficiency and performance), sporting goods (high-performance equipment), construction (reinforcement), and various other specialized engineering applications where reducing weight without sacrificing strength is paramount.

Carbon fiber production is a multi-stage process involving both chemical and mechanical steps. It begins with drawing long strands of precursor fibers, often made from polyacrylonitrile (PAN), rayon, or pitch. These fibers are then heated to very high temperatures in an oxygen-free environment, a process called carbonization. This intense heat causes the non-carbon atoms to be expelled, leaving behind tightly interlocked chains of carbon atoms. The resulting carbon fibers are then often treated to enhance their surface properties for better bonding with the resin matrix and coated with a sizing agent to improve handling and processing. A typical sequence for PAN-based carbon fiber involves spinning the precursor, stabilizing it through oxidation, carbonizing it at high temperatures, treating the surface to improve adhesion, and applying a sizing. The earliest form of carbon fiber can be traced back to 1879 when Thomas Edison carbonized cotton threads or bamboo slivers to create filaments for light bulbs. However, these were not high-performance fibers. In 1958, more advanced carbon fibers with around 20% carbon content were invented, though they had limited strength and stiffness. The true potential of carbon fiber's strength was realized in 1963 with the development of a new manufacturing process at a British research center.

Thomas Edison produced an early form of carbon fiber in 1879 for use in light bulbs. The development of high-performance carbon fibers in 1958 occurred near Cleveland, OH. The breakthrough in realizing carbon fiber's strength potential through a new manufacturing process took place at a British research center in 1963. Carbon fiber has enabled significant advancements in various industries due to its unique properties. In aerospace, it allows for lighter aircraft with improved fuel efficiency. In the automotive sector, it contributes to lighter, faster, and more fuel-efficient vehicles. Sporting goods like bicycles, golf clubs, and tennis rackets benefit from its high strength and low weight. Carbon fiber is also used in construction for reinforcing structures and in various other specialized applications where high strength-to-weight ratio and stiffness are crucial, contributing to improved performance and efficiency across multiple sectors.
