Graphene

Graphene is a revolutionary two-dimensional material

Overview

Graphene is a revolutionary two-dimensional material composed of a single layer of carbon atoms arranged in a honeycomb (hexagonal) lattice structure. It can also exist as a few coupled layers of this structure. Celebrated as the strongest, thinnest, and most conductive material known, graphene holds immense potential to unlock numerous applications across diverse fields, including electronics, energy storage, composite materials, medicine, and aerospace. Graphene's significance arises principally from its exceptional and unparalleled properties, making it highly promising for a wide range of technological applications. Its high electrical and thermal conductivity makes it ideal for advanced electronics and energy storage. Its remarkable strength and lightweight nature are valuable for creating novel composite materials.

While the theoretical study of graphene began much earlier, the first isolation of single-layer graphene was achieved through a mechanical exfoliation method at the University of Manchester. This involved using adhesive tape to repeatedly peel thin layers from a piece of graphite until a single layer was obtained. More scalable production methods are currently being developed, including chemical vapor deposition (CVD) on metal substrates and solution-based exfoliation. These methods aim to produce high-quality graphene in larger quantities for industrial applications. The theoretical study of graphene's electronic structure began in 1947 with the work of physicist Philip R. Wallace. The term "graphene" was coined in 1986.

The first isolation of single-layer graphene is credited to a team led by Andre Geim and Kostya Novoselov at the University of Manchester. Graphene's unique combination of properties has the potential to revolutionize numerous industries. In electronics, it could lead to faster, smaller, and more flexible devices. In energy, it promises more efficient solar cells and high-capacity batteries and supercapacitors. Its strength could enable lighter and stronger composite materials for aerospace and automotive applications. In medicine, its biocompatibility opens doors for drug delivery and biosensors. While still in the early stages of commercialization, graphene holds the key to significant technological advancements and new possibilities across various sectors.
