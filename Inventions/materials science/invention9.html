Short Description

Graphene is a groundbreaking two-dimensional material made of a single layer of carbon atoms in a hexagonal lattice. Known for being incredibly strong, thin, and highly conductive, it has vast potential in electronics, energy storage, composites, medicine, and aerospace.

Purpose

Graphene's importance stems from its extraordinary properties, including exceptional electrical and thermal conductivity, as well as remarkable strength and lightness. These qualities make it highly suitable for advanced electronics, energy storage, and innovative composite materials.

How It Is Made

While graphene had been studied theoretically for decades, its first successful isolation occurred in 2004 at the University of Manchester using a simple mechanical exfoliation techniqueâ€”peeling layers from graphite with adhesive tape until a single atomic layer was obtained. Since then, more scalable methods like chemical vapor deposition (CVD) and solution-based exfoliation have been developed to produce larger quantities of high-quality graphene for industrial use.

When It Is Made

The theoretical study of graphene's electronic structure began in 1947 with the work of physicist Philip R. Wallace. The term "graphene" was coined in 1986.

Who Made It

The first isolation of single-layer graphene is credited to a team led by Andre Geim and Kostya Novoselov at the University of Manchester.

Contribution To Society

Graphene's extraordinary properties could transform many industries. In electronics, it may enable faster and more flexible devices. In energy, it offers promise for high-capacity batteries, supercapacitors, and more efficient solar cells. Its strength and lightness are ideal for advanced composites in aerospace and automotive sectors. In medicine, its biocompatibility allows for innovative uses like drug delivery and biosensors. Though still early in commercialization, graphene is poised to drive major technological breakthroughs.
