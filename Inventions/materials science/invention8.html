Short Description

A superconductor is a material that, when cooled below a critical temperature, conducts electricity with zero resistance, allowing current to flow endlessly. It also exhibits the Meissner effect, expelling magnetic fields and enabling magnetic levitation.

Purpose

Superconductors enable lossless electrical transmission and powerful magnetic fields. This benefits efficient power grids, MRI machines, particle accelerators, maglev trains, and sensitive sensors. Their ability to carry large currents without energy loss allows for smaller, more efficient devices.

How It Is Made

Superconductivity occurs when materials are cooled below a critical temperature, causing electrons to form Cooper pairs that move without resistance. This fragile state can be disrupted by heat or strong magnetic fields. Classical superconductors like mercury, tin, and lead become superconductive near absolute zero, needing costly cryogenic cooling.

When It Is Made

Superconductivity was discovered in 1911 by Dutch physicist Heike Kamerlingh Onnes, who found that mercury's electrical resistance disappeared at cryogenic temperatures. He later observed the same effect in tin and lead, now known as classical superconductors.

Who Made It

The discovery of superconductivity is credited to Heike Kamerlingh Onnes, a Dutch physicist.

Contribution To Society

Superconductors have the potential to transform society by enabling ultra-efficient energy transmission and powerful magnetic fields. They're key to technologies like MRI machines, particle accelerators, and maglev trains. Ongoing research into high-temperature superconductors could make these applications more practical and lead to innovations in energy storage, electronics, and beyond.
