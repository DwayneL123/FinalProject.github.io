Short Description

The Bronze Age (c. 2000–700 BC) marked a major technological advance as societies began using bronze—a copper and tin alloy—for tools, weapons, and art. Stronger and more versatile than stone, bronze revolutionized agriculture, warfare, and craftsmanship. Its production and trade contributed to economic growth and social development.

Purpose

Bronze served primarily to create more effective tools for agriculture, such as plows that increased crop yields, and deadlier weapons and armor that revolutionized warfare, shifting the balance of power to those equipped with bronze.

How It Is Made

Bronze was made by melting and mixing tin and copper, then casting the molten metal into molds. Settlements either had resident bronzesmiths or relied on traveling ones who traded bronze items. These skilled metalworkers used techniques like hammering, embossing, inlaying, and gilding—many still used today.

When It Is Made

The Bronze Age generally lasted from around 2000 BC to 700 BC.

Who Made It

Bronze artifacts were crafted by skilled metalworkers known as bronzesmiths. These artisans played a crucial role in Bronze Age societies, with some being attached to specific settlements and others operating as itinerant traders and craftspeople.

Contribution To Society

Bronze technology revolutionized ancient societies by enabling stronger tools for farming and crafts, and more effective weapons that transformed warfare. Its production and trade boosted economies, encouraged social complexity, and advanced art through decorative metalwork.
