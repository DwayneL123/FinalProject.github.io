Short Description

Kevlar, a synthetic aramid fiber known as poly-para-phenylene terephthalamide, is lightweight, exceptionally strong, and resistant to heat and corrosion. Woven into textiles or composites, it offers great durability and protection, making it popular in aerospace, body armor, car brakes, and sports gear.

Purpose

Kevlar is a strong, lightweight material offering superior protection and reinforcement. Its high strength-to-weight ratio suits body armor and bulletproof vests, while heat and corrosion resistance benefit aerospace and automotive uses. Kevlar’s toughness also reinforces composites in aircraft, boats, and sports gear, boosting performance and safety.

How It Is Made

Kevlar, a nylon-like polymer developed by DuPont, gains its strength and stiffness from highly oriented polymer chains. It’s made through a condensation reaction between para-phenylenediamine and terephthaloyl chloride, producing long chains spun into fibers. These fibers are woven into textiles or embedded in resins for composites and can be combined with other fibers to create hybrid materials with custom properties.

When It Is Made

Kevlar was first produced and commercially introduced by the DuPont company in 1971.

Who Made It

Kevlar was developed by the DuPont chemical company as a groundbreaking synthetic fiber.

Contribution To Society

Kevlar has improved safety and performance in many fields. It’s vital in body armor for law enforcement and military, helps make lighter, fuel-efficient aircraft, and strengthens automotive tires and brakes. Kevlar also protects workers with durable clothing and enhances sports gear for better performance.
