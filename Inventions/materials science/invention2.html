Short Description

Iron and steel, key ferrous metals, became essential during the Second Industrial Revolution (from ~1850). Steel, an iron-carbon alloy, rose in importance for its high strength and low cost, making it ideal for construction and industry. Their strength comes from iron's crystal structure and how carbon and other elements harden it by restricting internal movement.

Purpose

: Instead of being rare and expensive, iron and especially steel became the go-to materials for building big things like bridges, tall buildings, and railroads. They were also essential for making machines and tools, as well as for building ships and trains. Steel, being stronger than earlier types of iron, allowed for even bigger and tougher structures and more powerful machines, really driving the advancements of industry.

How It Is Made

Before mass production, steel was expensive and made in small batches. This changed with Henry Bessemerâ€™s 1855 invention of the Bessemer converter, which blew air through molten pig iron to burn off excess carbon. The heat from this reaction kept the metal molten, allowing for faster, cheaper steel production. Later improvements enhanced consistency.

When It Is Made

While the Second Industrial Revolution, characterized by the rise of steel, began around 1850, a reliable method for the mass manufacture of steel wasn't invented until the 1860s with Bessemer's process, becoming widely adopted in the 1870s. Before this period, steel was an expensive specialty product.

Who Made It

The key figure in revolutionizing steel production was Henry Bessemer, who introduced the Bessemer converter in 1855 at his steelworks in Sheffield, England. His invention provided the first cost-effective method for massproducing steel.

Contribution To Society

Cheap, mass-produced steel transformed society. It enabled the construction of skyscrapers, large bridges, railways, stronger machines, and better ships. As a core material, steel drove major technological and societal advances in the late 1800s and 1900s. Its impact is still seen today in modern infrastructure and industry.
